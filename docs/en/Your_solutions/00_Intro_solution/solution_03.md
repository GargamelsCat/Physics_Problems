# Proportionality in Physics: Universal Law of Gravitation

## Problem Statement
Consider the **Universal Law of Gravitation**:

$$
F = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

Where:
* $F$ is the gravitational force.
* $G$ is the gravitational constant.
* $m_1$ and $m_2$ are the two masses.
* $r$ is the distance between their centers.

**Determine the factor by which the force $F$ changes if the distance $r$ is *doubled* and both masses ($m_1$ and $m_2$) are *halved*.**

---

## Solution

To find the change in force, we compare the initial force ($F_{old}$) with the new force ($F_{new}$) after applying the changes.

[Image of Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation diagram showing two masses m1 and m2 separated by distance r]

### 1. Define the Initial State
The initial force is given by the standard formula:

$$
F_{old} = G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

### 2. Define the New Variables
According to the problem, we apply the following transformations:
* New masses: $m_1' = \frac{1}{2}m_1$ and $m_2' = \frac{1}{2}m_2$
* New distance: $r' = 2r$

### 3. Substitute into the Gravitation Formula
Now, we plug these new values into the force equation to find $F_{new}$:

$$
F_{new} = G \frac{(\frac{1}{2}m_1) (\frac{1}{2}m_2)}{(2r)^2}
$$

### 4. Simplify the Expression
First, simplify the numerator and the denominator separately:
* **Numerator:** $\frac{1}{2} \times \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$
* **Denominator:** $(2r)^2 = 4r^2$

Substituting these back:

$$
F_{new} = G \frac{\frac{1}{4} m_1 m_2}{4r^2}
$$

Now, combine the numerical constants:

$$
F_{new} = \left(\frac{1}{4}\right) \times \left(\frac{1}{4}\right) G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2}
$$

which simplifies to

$$
F_{new} = \frac{1}{16} \, G \frac{m_1 m_2}{r^2} = \frac{1}{16} F_{old}.
$$
## Final Answer
The gravitational force decreases by a factor of **16**. In other words, the new force is **1/16th** of the original force.
